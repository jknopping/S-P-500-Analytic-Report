Capstone 1: Evaluating the Five-Year Performance of the S&P 500

1.0 Introduction

There are a large number of index funds (a type of investment fund based on a preset collection of stocks) in the American stock market for an investor to consider. The most notable of these indexes include the Dow Jones Industrial Average, the Standard & Poor's (S&P) 500, and the Nasdaq 100. While all three indexes can be similar, the S&P 500 contains the largest 500 companies in the United States, compared to only 30 and 100 companies in the Dow Industrial and Nasdaq, respectively (Royal, 2019).

Therefore, the S&P 500 is considered to be a diversified, valubale representation for overall market performace. The index includes companies from a wide range of sectors, as compared to other market indexes. For reference, the breakdown by sectors was as follows in 2017: information technology (24.9%), financials (14.7%), health care (13.7%), consumer discretionary (12.7 %), industrials (10.2%), consumer staples (7.7%), energy (5.7%), utilities (2.9%), materials (2.9%), real estate (2.8%), and telecom services (1.9%)(Amadeo, 2019).

In this analytical research report, the performance of the S&P 500 over the past five years will be evaluated to examine any exisitng trends, and whether or not investing in S&P 500 is an advisable strategy for investors.

1.1 Why is this data significant?

According to indsutry experts, including the renowned Warren Buffet, the S&P 500 represents a simple solution for individual investors looking for an edge (Royal, 2019). While it hasn't occured every year over the past ten, the S&P 500 generally returns approximately 10 percent a year (Amadeo, 2019). Therefore, buying and holding an index fund based on the S&P 500 is a safe and effective strategy for investors, according to many financial advisors.

Given its potential as an investing strategy for individuals, data that provides insight into the trends of S&P 500 index funds can be extremely valuable. Moreover, providing context to recent trends based on recent historical data can help investors make decisons, and understand if recent performance can be considered normal.

Lastly, the S&P 500 is regarded as a valuable indicator for overall market performance and movement (Kenton, 2019). Better understanding the S&P 500 through this dataset will also help provide information for the market in general.

1.2 The Data

The data collected for this analytic report was obtained from the Yahoo! Finance webpage ("S&P 500 (^GSPC) Historical Data", 2019). The data includes available historical data for the S&P 500 (^GSPC) over the past five years, beginning 10/27/2014 and ending 10/14/2019.

Yahoo! Finance allows for weekly data to be downloaded as a CSV file. This download was performed, and the CSV file was read into a data frame.

The CSV file obtained from Yahoo! Finance includes seven columns of data: date, open, high, low, close, adjusted close, and volume. In the context of this dataset, these categories provide the following information:

-Open: the value of the index fund at the start of the trading week
-High: the highest value of the index fund during the trading week
-Low: the lowest value of the index fund during the trading week
-Close: the value of the index fund at the end of the trading week
-Adjusted (Adj) Close: a more complex analysis of the closing value that takes into account other factors such as dividends, stock splits and new stock offerings
-Volume: the number of shares/contracts traded during the trading week

It should be noted that the close and adjusted close values were the same for any given week in this set of data.

As demonstrated by the skew in the histogram below, the distribution of the S&P 500 data set is not normal; the majority of values are not close to the value of the mean. Therefore, summary statistics (i.e. mean, quartile ranges, etc.) are not very useful, as they are not measures of central tendency for this distribution. A possible explanation for the skew is that the value of the S&P 500 is not an independent variable in this scenario. The value is likely to be closer to its value of the previous week. However, the percent change in value for the S&P 500 is likely to show a more normal distribution.

As theorized above, a histogram using % change does represent a normal distribution. In order for a distribution to be considered normal, the data must follow the "empirical rule" (defined below). Therefore, summary statistics for the percent change in the value of the S&P 500 stock provide accurate, useful information. For example, the average change in stock price was +0.15% per week over the past five years.

-The "empirical rule": For a variable to be classified as normally-distributed, the following criteria must be met: ~68% of values are within 1 standard deviation of the mean, 95% of values are within two standard deviations of the mean, and 99.7% of values are within 3 standard deviations of the mean

4.0 Conclusion

The analysis of weekly data for the S&P 500 provided useful information that will help individual investors make decisons on how to strategically manage their money. The strategy of buying and holding an index fund based on the S&P 500 did in fact prove to be a safe and effective strategy for investors, given that the value of the fund has increased 65.73% over the past five years. While there are certainly periodic dips in value, the S&P 500 appears to steadily grow over a long period of time. Therefore, it is advisable to be patient with any index funds an indivual owns, rather than to sell when the stock begins performing poorly; in all instances over the past five years, the value recovered and continued to grow. In addition, the stock appears relatively stable, as the average percent change in stock value has been usually within two percent over any given week, with an average increase of 0.15%.

Furthermore, the visual data from the figure in Section 3.3 helped identify a potential trend. The graphs in the figure indicate a tendency for the value of the S&P 500 to drop from the last quarter to the first quarter of the calendar year. Given this trend, individual investors may want to reconsider investing in the stock this time of the year. However, this decison to wait for an annual market dip before investing is no guarantee based on this set of data, and investing in a S&P 500 index fund has proved profitable year-round. This decision can be left to the indivual, but the trend proves useful nonetheless, especially given the S&P 500's regard as a indicator for overally market performance.

4.1 Further Research

After analyzing the performance of the S&P 500 over the past five years, there are several areas of research that would be worth exploring further.

As mentioned in the introduction, the S&P 500 is one of the most important indexes in the American stock market. Because it contains 500 of the largest US companies, it is considered to be one of the best indicators of overall market performance. However, both the Dow Jones Industrial Average and the Nasdaq 100 are also very popular indexes, much in the same way. It would be worth exploring how these indexes compare to the S&P 500 in terms of long-term performance and market stability. Which of these three indexes represents the best choice for investors looking for long-term gains? All three of those indexes are comprised of large-cap companies, how would an index comprised of small-cap companies (i.e. Russell 2000 index) differ (Chen, 2019)? The answers to these questions would help guide long-term investing strategies.

Another area worth exploring is the trend for a regression in the S&P 500's value over the last and first quarters of the calendar year. Is this occurence more specific to the past five years, or is it representative of a larger trend? Given the discussed significance of the S&P 500, the answer to this question has large implications for investors and the American economy as a whole.
